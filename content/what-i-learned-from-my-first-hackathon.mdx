# What I Learned From My First Hackathon

BV Hacks 1.0 was my first hackathon, and it became one of the most memorable learning experiences in my development journey. Before participating, I had an idea of what hackathons were: team-based events where people solve problems and build solutions within a limited time. But experiencing one firsthand was very different from simply knowing the format.

It was exciting, fast-paced, and a little intimidating at first. At the same time, it showed me how much growth can happen when you work under pressure with a team and a clear goal.

## Understanding the problem before chasing the solution

One of the first things I learned was that good problem-solving starts with understanding the problem clearly. In a hackathon, it is easy to jump quickly into features or technology choices. But if the team does not fully understand the actual need, even a technically strong solution can feel weak.

During BV Hacks 1.0, I saw how important it was to:

- Break down the problem statement carefully
- Identify who the solution is for
- Think about what pain points matter most
- Focus on practicality instead of overcomplicating the idea

That mindset helped me see hackathons not just as coding events, but as problem-solving events where clarity matters as much as implementation.

## Working with a team in a fast-moving environment

Since it was my first hackathon, collaboration was one of the biggest learning areas for me. Working with teammates in a time-bound environment is very different from casually discussing ideas. Everyone needs to contribute, communicate clearly, and stay aligned with the goal.

I learned that teamwork in a hackathon works best when:

- Responsibilities are clear
- Communication stays simple and direct
- People listen to each other during brainstorming
- The team avoids spending too much time debating minor decisions

Even when not everyone agrees on every idea, momentum matters. I realized that strong teamwork is often about moving forward together rather than waiting for a perfect plan.

## Managing time constraints

Time pressure changes how you think. In my first hackathon, I quickly understood that time management is one of the most important skills in the entire event.

There is rarely enough time to do everything you imagine at the beginning. That means teams must prioritize:

- Which idea is realistic within the event timeline
- Which features are essential
- What can be simplified
- What is best saved for future improvements

This was one of the most practical lessons I took away. In normal learning environments, you sometimes have the flexibility to keep refining and expanding. In a hackathon, you need to make trade-offs, stay focused, and keep progressing.

## Confidence grows through participation

Before my first hackathon, I was unsure how well I would perform in a competitive environment. I wondered whether I had enough experience, whether I would be able to contribute meaningfully, and whether I could handle the pace.

By the end of the event, one of the biggest takeaways for me was confidence. Not confidence from winning, but confidence from participating fully, thinking through problems, collaborating with others, and staying engaged throughout the process.

I learned that you do not need to know everything before joining an event like this. Growth often comes from stepping into unfamiliar situations and learning while you are in them.

## What the experience taught me

BV Hacks 1.0 helped me improve in several areas at once:

- Problem analysis
- Idea generation
- Team collaboration
- Communication
- Time management
- Presentation thinking

It also helped me understand the broader value of hackathons. They are not just about competition. They are also about exposure, learning, adaptability, and experiencing how ideas move from discussion to execution.

## A milestone in my journey

Because it was my first hackathon, BV Hacks 1.0 feels like an important milestone. It introduced me to a more practical and collaborative side of development. It taught me that building under pressure requires both technical thinking and calm decision-making.

Most importantly, it motivated me to keep participating in hackathons and to keep improving. The event showed me that even when the outcome is not a winning position, the experience itself can be incredibly valuable.

My first hackathon did not just teach me how hackathons work. It helped me become more comfortable with challenges, more open to teamwork, and more confident in my ability to grow through real experience.
